# How to Write Your Candidate Profile

> **Where the file is:** start from the blank template at
> **`config/example/candidate_profile.template.md`** (and the optional
> `candidate_addendum.template.md` beside it). Copy it, fill it in, and save your
> version as **`candidate_profile.md`** in your config folder. There's also a
> complete filled-in example in that same `config/example/` folder you can read
> for reference.
>
> **Don't want to start from a blank page?** Use the
> [copy-paste profile-builder prompt](profile-builder-prompt.md): paste it plus
> your resume into Claude or ChatGPT and it drafts the whole thing in this format.

> **About the file format:** The template files end in `.md` (Markdown). They're plain text files. You can open and edit them in any text editor — Notepad on Windows, TextEdit on Mac, Google Docs, or even Word. You don't need any special software.

Your candidate profile is the single most important file in this tool. Every job gets scored against it. A vague profile produces vague scores. A specific, honest profile produces scores you can actually trust.

This guide walks you through each section of the profile template and explains how the scorer uses it.

---

## The short version

The scorer reads each job description alongside your profile and asks: "How well does this person fit this role?" It looks for alignment between what the job needs and what you bring, flags gaps, checks compensation and location, and assigns a score from 1 to 10.

Three things in your profile have outsized impact on scoring:

1. **Hard blockers** cap the score at 4. If a job requires something you listed as a hard blocker, it can never score above 4 no matter how well everything else matches.

2. **Unique match signals** lift the score. If the job posting contains language that matches your signals (e.g. "build from scratch," "first hire," "growth-stage"), the scorer weights this positively.

3. **Soft gaps** are noted but not penalised heavily. The scorer acknowledges them without treating them as dealbreakers.

Everything else (your experience, strengths, target roles, compensation, location) provides the context the scorer needs to make a reasonable judgment.

## Section by section

### Summary
2-3 sentences about who you are. Think of it as the version of yourself you'd describe to a recruiter in an elevator. Keep it factual: years of experience, what kind of work you do, what you're looking for next. The scorer uses this to quickly understand your professional identity.

### Career Timeline
Your work history in reverse chronological order. For each role, focus on what you owned, the scale you operated at, and what you accomplished. "Managed CRM" is less useful than "Administered HubSpot for a 200-person sales org, built all reporting dashboards, and trained 15 new users." The scorer matches specific experience against job requirements, so specificity matters.

Recent and relevant roles deserve more detail. Older or less relevant roles can be a line or two.

### Key Strengths
5-8 things you're genuinely good at. Be concrete. The scorer compares these directly against the requirements in each job posting. "Strong communicator" tells the scorer nothing. "Translating technical concepts into training materials for non-technical audiences" tells it a lot.

### Target Roles
The roles you actually want, listed in priority order. This helps the scorer weight its assessment. A job that perfectly matches your #1 target role scores higher than one that matches your #5. If you've deprioritised certain role types (and have a reason), note that too, so the scorer doesn't give them undeserved high marks.

### Hard Blockers
This is the most important section to get right. Hard blockers cap the score at 4. Be honest about what you genuinely cannot do right now.

Common hard blockers include: specific technical skills you don't have (SQL, Python, a particular platform), seniority levels that are too high (director/VP at a large company when you haven't managed teams), domain expertise you lack (healthcare, fintech, cybersecurity), or degree requirements you don't meet.

Don't list things as blockers if they're merely areas of weakness. A gap you could address in 3-6 months is a soft gap, not a hard blocker. A skill the job requires you to demonstrate in an interview tomorrow is a hard blocker.

### Unique Match Signals
The kinds of roles and environments where you do your best work. These are your "light up" indicators. When the scorer sees these in a job posting, it gives the job a boost.

Think about: company stage and size, culture markers (remote-first, async, transparent), role characteristics (build from scratch, cross-functional, high autonomy), industry or mission alignment, and any specific language in job postings that has historically excited you.

### Soft Gaps
Real gaps that you're aware of but that aren't dealbreakers. Being upfront about these helps the scorer give accurate scores rather than inflated ones. If you don't list your soft gaps, the scorer might flag them as bigger issues than they are. If you list them with context, the scorer can note them proportionally.

### Compensation
Your target range, the currency, and any structural notes. If you know the difference between contractor and salaried compensation in your market, note it. If you're flexible on compensation for the right role, say so. The scorer uses this to flag jobs that are clearly below or above your range.

### Location and Commute
Where you are, what you're open to, and what's a dealbreaker. Be specific about remote policies: "Remote-US only" might or might not work for a Canadian applicant depending on the company's contractor policies. If you know these nuances for your situation, spell them out.

### Education and Certifications
Straightforward. List what you have, note anything in progress. If you're self-taught in areas that typically require formal training, say so. Some job postings list degrees as "required" vs "preferred" and the scorer handles these differently.

### Tools and Systems
Group by proficiency level. "Admin-level HubSpot" is a very different signal than "familiar with HubSpot." If you're actively learning a tool, list it as learning. The scorer uses this to check tool requirements in job postings.

## Tips

**Start from your resume.** If you already have an up-to-date resume, paste it into Claude, ChatGPT, or whatever AI tool you prefer and ask: "Rewrite this into the candidate profile template format." Then review and refine. The AI will get the structure right; you make sure the content is accurate.

**Be honest, not modest.** The profile is private. Nobody sees it but you and the scorer. If you built something impressive, say so with specifics. If you have a real gap, name it. Honesty in both directions produces better scores.

**Update the addendum, not the main profile.** When your targeting changes (and it will), use the addendum file to record the updates rather than rewriting your main profile. The addendum overrides the main profile where they conflict, so you keep a clean history of your original positioning while adapting your scoring.

**Review your scores after the first run.** If jobs you think should score high are scoring low (or vice versa), the issue is almost always in your profile. Adjust your hard blockers, match signals, or target roles and re-run.
