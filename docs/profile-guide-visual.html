Don't describe tasks. Describe capabilities.

The scorer matches capabilities to job requirements. What your work required you to be good at matters more than what you called it.

Try this
For each role you've held, describe what you did day to day in plain language, as if telling a friend. Then pull out the capabilities. Those go in your profile.

Know what kind of gap you're looking at.

Three kinds. They affect scoring very differently.

Hard Blocker

"Could I show this in an interview next week?"

No. The job needs SQL and you've never written a query. It needs 10 direct reports and you've had zero.

Score capped at 4

Soft Gap

"Could I learn this in 3-6 months?"

Yes. You've only worked at small venues. You know Toast POS well but haven't touched Aloha.

Noted, not penalised

Not a Gap

"Have I done this under a different name?"

Yes. Single employer but five progressive roles. No formal title but you did the work.

Don't list it. Explain it.

Ground your match signals in evidence.

Describe where you've ALREADY done your best work, not where you think you'd like to work.

Aspiration

"I think I'd thrive remotely."

"I'm interested in growth-stage companies."

"I like building from scratch."

Evidence

"I've worked remotely for four years. My best outcomes happened during that time."

"I joined at 12 people and helped build to 40. That pace is where I'm most effective."

"Every process my team uses was built by me from nothing."

Try this
For each match signal, write one sentence of proof. If you can't, it's an aspiration, not a signal. It might still belong in your profile, but label it honestly.

Speak the employer's language, not yours.

Most companies use systems (ATS) that scan your resume for specific terms before a human ever sees it. You might have the right experience described in the wrong words.

Yours
"Worked across teams to keep projects moving"
Theirs
"Cross-functional stakeholder management"
Yours
"Set up the POS and kept inventory counts accurate"
Theirs
"POS administration and inventory integrity management"
Yours
"Helped new hires get up to speed on the floor"
Theirs
"Staff onboarding and training programme management"
Yours
"Made guides so people could figure things out themselves"
Theirs
"Self-serve enablement content and knowledge base development"

Same work. Different vocabulary. The ATS scans for their version, not yours. When the scorer flags a job as a strong fit (7+), it includes the specific keywords you should make sure appear in your resume for that posting.

Try this
Look at 3-5 job postings for your target roles. What words and phrases keep showing up? Those are your industry's ATS keywords. Make sure your profile uses them naturally instead of your casual descriptions.

Shallow profile vs. strong profile.

Same person. Same experience. The difference is depth.

Shallow

Summary

"Hospitality professional with 7 years of restaurant experience."

Strength

"Strong communicator"

Target

"Operations roles"

Blocker

(none listed)

Signal

"I like fast-paced environments"

Strong

Summary

"Restaurant manager with 7 years running multi-location venues where I built every staff-facing process from onboarding to scheduling. I create the structure so everyone else can focus on the guests."

Strength

"Turning standard procedures into training materials for new and seasonal staff (40+ guides, 6 onboarding modules)"

Target

"1. Operations Coordinator, 2. Training & Development, 3. Program Coordinator"

Blocker

"Excel pivot tables: currently learning, couldn't pass a live exercise today."

Signal

"Opened a new location from day one and built every process still in use. Ground-up roles are where I do my best work."

The strong version gives the scorer something to work with. Every section connects to real experience with specific details.
